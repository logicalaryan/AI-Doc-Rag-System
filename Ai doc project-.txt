Chunking-
What is chunking?

Chunking-Chunking in AI is the process of breaking down large pieces of text into smaller, manageable, and semantically meaningful segments called "chunks"

Advantages-
1.Accuracy-high
2.cost-less
3.Precision of sources-
Without chunking, the LLM says "according to your document...". With chunking, you can point to the exact paragraph — "Source: annual_report.pdf, page 14". This is critical for trust and debugging hallucinations.

Embeddings-
1.An embedding is a mathematical technique that translates complex, real-world data—like words, images, or audio—into sequences of numbers called vectors.

An embedding model maps data points as coordinates in a high-dimensional space
Semantic Proximity: Items that are alike or share similar contexts are placed close to each other in this space, while unrelated items are farther apart

Measuring Distance: AI algorithms use mathematical formulas to calculate the distance between these vectors. This allows the computer to determine if two sentences, products, or images have the same underlying meaning

Example-Platforms like Netflix or Spotify use embeddings to suggest movies or songs that "feel" similar to what you already like .


ChromaDB-
ChromaDB (often just called Chroma) is an open-source vector database designed specifically to store and search embeddings in AI applications.

Traditional databases (like SQL) are built for strings and numbers. They struggle to store high-dimensional embedding vectors (which look like long lists of numbers, e.g., [0.12, -0.43, 0.91...]). ChromaDB fixes this by specializing in similarity searches

Long-Term Memory for LLMs: Large Language Models like ChatGPT have no built-in memory of your personal data. ChromaDB acts as an external hard drive where an AI can store and look up information.

Instead of matching words, you give ChromaDB a phrase, and it returns the closest matching text based on mathematical meaning .

who decides the value of k?
   When a user asks a question, the question is converted into an embedding. The retriever compares this embedding with all the document embeddings stored in the vector database and ranks the documents based on similarity. I configure the value of k, for example k = 5, so the retriever returns the top 5 most relevant documents. These documents are then passed to the LLM, which reads them and generates the final answer.

User asks a question.
Retriever fetches the top k documents.
Hit@k checks whether at least one of those documents is relevant.
If you're evaluating the retriever, record:
1 if there is a relevant document.
0 otherwise.

Hit Rate is the average of Hit@k across a set of queries, indicating the percentage of queries for which the retriever successfully returned at least one relevant document in the top-k results.
MRR(Mean reciprocal rank)-finds the position of the first retrieved document.1/rank
